Welcome, Sezen. This platform holds your entire Statistics 2 course, arranged the way your textbooks are — Week 2 through Week 8 — so that each screen teaches one idea with a picture, not a wall of text. Statistics 2 is where psychology becomes testable: almost every claim you will read for the rest of your degree ("this therapy works", "this trait predicts that outcome") is, underneath, one of the procedures in here. Read visually. Look at the diagram first and let it tell the story; then read the five bullets beside it as captions, not as new material; then lock the idea in with the memory hook. One thread runs through all seven weeks: every test is really answering the same question — "could what I saw in my sample have happened by chance alone?" — and a result is only ever as trustworthy as the design that produced it. Keep that in view and the eight-week arc becomes one continuous argument.

Learning Objectives
  • Build and read confidence intervals and significance tests for one and two proportions, and apply the plus-four fix.
  • State the pillars of research ethics — Nuremberg, Belmont, the APA code, the IRB, informed consent, deception, animal welfare.
  • Construct cross-tabs and run chi-square tests of independence and goodness-of-fit with the right degrees of freedom.
  • Separate correlation from causation; diagnose the bidirectional and third-variable problems and Simpson's paradox.
  • Fit and test simple and multiple regression, interpret slopes, R², and the four kinds of validity.
  • Run a one-way ANOVA by comparing between-group with within-group variance, then follow up with contrasts and multiple comparisons.
  • Analyse a factorial experiment with two-way ANOVA, separating two main effects from their interaction.
  • Recognise repeated-measures designs, check sphericity with Mauchly's test, correct it, and report an effect size.
  • Above all — match the right test to the right design, and say honestly what each result can and cannot claim.
Foundational Terms — the cross-cutting vocabulary

These span the whole course. Each week also has its OWN Key Terms table — open a week in the Lessons tab to see it.

TermWorking definition
Population vs SampleThe whole group you care about vs the subset you actually measure. Inference speaks about the first using only the second.
Parameter vs StatisticA parameter (p, μ) is the fixed, unknown truth about the population; a statistic (p̂, x̄) is its estimate from the sample.
Confidence intervalA range of plausible values for a parameter, built so a stated % of such intervals would trap the true value.
H₀ / HₐH₀ = 'nothing is going on' (no effect/difference/relationship). Hₐ = the researcher's claim that something is.
p-valueProbability of data at least this extreme if H₀ were true. Small p = your data would be surprising under 'nothing'.
Type I / II errorType I (α): false alarm — reject a true H₀. Type II (β): a miss — keep a false H₀. Power = 1 − β.
Categorical vs QuantitativeCategorical = groups (job / no job). Quantitative = an amount (reaction time). This choice dictates the test.
Confounding variableA variable tangled with the cause so their effects can't be separated — the great destroyer of causal claims.
Standard error (SE)How much a statistic would wobble across repeated samples — the engine inside every interval and test.
Degrees of freedom (df)How many values are free to vary; it selects the exact reference curve (t, χ², F) you read the p-value from.
Effect sizeHow BIG the effect is, independent of n (r, R², ω²). Significance says 'real'; effect size says 'how much'.

Synthesis — the thread through all seven weeks

Everything in Statistics 2 is one escalating attempt to separate signal from noise while staying honest about what the data may claim. We begin with the humblest signal — a single proportion — wrap it in a confidence interval, and learn that inference is only as sound as the random sample and the ethics behind it. Categories then multiply into cross-tabs, where chi-square measures the distance between what we saw and what mere independence predicts. Moving to quantities, correlation shows two variables travelling together — and insists that travelling together is not causing, because a reversed arrow or a lurking third variable makes the identical pattern. Regression draws the line through that cloud and judges each predictor "holding the others constant" — a statistical echo of experimental control — while validity reminds us a flawless model can still describe a worthless study. The ANOVA family then generalises the comparison of means: one-way ANOVA weighs between-group against within-group variance; two-way ANOVA adds a second factor and the pivotal idea of interaction; and repeated-measures ANOVA lets people be their own controls, trading independence for sphericity. Across all of it: the design chooses the test, the p-value only flags the improbable, and the causal claim is earned by how carefully the study was built — never by arithmetic alone.
